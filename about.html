  • 2015 My First Python Script

    This is the first time I've experienced programming!

    It was way back in my Secondary School IT extra-curriculum. Tasked to create something cool with Python, I created a short Minecraft Visual Novel with RenPy and screenshots from the game.

    At that time, it didn't really spark my interest too much, but it was cool to create a "Cookie Clicker"-like game using a trivial infinite event while loop. It's rather easy to entertain a teenager with big numbers.

  • 2017 My First Program in C++

    I got into this Vertical Scrolling Rhythm Game (VSRG) called osu!mania. At its infancy, it didn't have tools that creators (known as mappers) could leverage. Aiming to clear this gap, and to try something challenging, I took up the challenge of coding in C++, one of the more complicated languages to learn.

    I created sv-tool, a terminal tool that helps create scrolling speed modifiers in bulk.

    This is also my first GitHub public repository!
  • 2019 My first ML Research Paper try

    Following my passion for VSRGs , I wanted to get one foot into ML by solving one of the most complicated problems that my genre of games always had: Predicting Difficulty of a Level.

    I won't go too deep into why it's difficult, but in retrospect, it's a combination of ML skillsets from NLP , CV , Collaborative Filtering, and, to bring it all together, Software Engineering.

    You can take a look at the project ppshift_ml and the mini mock research paper on the subject

    It's no surprise that it's not the best approach, however, it gave me a good idea on what ML research can entail. This experience made me more confident in doing more.

  • 2019 LaTeX for fancy documents

    Next, I created a small crash course LaTeX document on how to mathematically approach scroll speed modification in osu!mania. SV Crash Course

    It's definitely not one for those who don't play VSRGs

  • 2019 R Lang, for the lazy Pythonistas

    At that time, R Lang and Python were head to head, challenging each other to rule the world of Data Science.

    Both then, (and also now), are extremely accessible options for beginners, and prove to be great investments of time, as they are versatile languages for many data engineering use cases.

    My curious mind wanted to know what's the haps, thus created vsrgtools, a simple R Lang library that deals with I/O and trivial data analytics for osu!mania maps.

    After giving it a try for a while, I can definitely see how accessible it is, however, the only edge it had over Python was the ease of plotting complicated graphs through the marvelous ggplot2. It's just not enough to keep me in R, especially when R consistently underperforms Python in performance for ML, larger datasets, and honestly, its editor support is subpar.
